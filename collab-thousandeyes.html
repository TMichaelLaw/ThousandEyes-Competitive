§ 02 — Know The Field

Six rivals, two markets. Each leaves the network blind.

Walk in respecting what each rival has built — they all have legitimate observability stories at the application layer. The seam is identical across all of them: none owns a network intelligence platform, so all of them depend on third-party monitoring tools like IR, Nectar, Operata, and VOSS to fill the gap.

Collaboration · UCaaS rivals
Microsoft
Microsoft Teams
Call Quality Dashboard · Teams Admin Center
What it is

Teams CQD provides per-call quality scores, network metrics from the Teams client, and Microsoft 365 Admin Center reporting. Strong inside Microsoft's own telemetry universe — endpoint network adapter info, jitter, packet loss as measured by the client.

The seam

CQD reports what the Teams client experienced. It cannot independently verify whether the ISP, DNS, BGP, or the path between the user and the Teams cloud is at fault. Microsoft has no equivalent of ThousandEyes. Customers routinely buy IR, Nectar, or VOSS specifically to get end-to-end visibility CQD doesn't provide.

"Telemetry from Teams, not telemetry of the network beneath Teams."
Zoom
Zoom Workplace + Phone
Zoom QSS · Zoom Dashboard
What it is

Zoom Quality of Service Subscription (QSS) and the Zoom Dashboard provide call quality, device, and meeting-level analytics. Pretty UI, decent depth — but again, the data is what Zoom's client measured, fed back into Zoom's own analytics.

The seam

Zoom owns no network intelligence platform. No BGP visibility. No global cloud agent mesh outside its own data centers. Zoom customers commonly deploy Operata or Nectar for true end-to-end observability — adding cost, integration overhead, and another vendor relationship just to reach parity with what Cisco ships in the box.

"Pretty dashboards. Same architectural ceiling as Teams."
Customer Experience · CCaaS rivals
Genesys
Genesys Cloud CX
Built-in monitoring + AppFoundry partners
What it is

Genesys has its own monitoring snapshots tool for agents, edges, trunks, websites. The AppFoundry marketplace lists IR, Nectar, VOSS, Operata, and others as observability extensions — explicitly because the platform itself doesn't ship deep network intelligence.

The seam

Genesys partners aggressively with third-party monitoring vendors precisely because the platform alone cannot answer "why is my Genesys Cloud agent's voice quality bad right now and is it our network or theirs?" Customers pay twice — once for Genesys, once for the observability layer.

"Strong CX features. The network observability is somebody else's product."
NICE
NICE CXone
CXone Mpower analytics + partners
What it is

NICE CXone is a strong CCaaS platform with deep WFM, QM, and analytics. Network monitoring relies on agent-side telemetry surfaced in CXone's reporting plus integrations with Nectar, IR, and others.

The seam

Same pattern as Genesys. Network and internet path visibility comes from a partner ecosystem, not from NICE itself. The agent's experience can be diagnosed; the path between agent and CXone cloud cannot — without buying additional tools.

"Top-tier CX. Network intelligence rented from third parties."
Five9
Five9 Intelligent CX
VCC + integrations
What it is

Five9 is a credible mid-market CCaaS with strong AI agent and IVA capabilities. Voice quality monitoring exists at the platform level. Like the others, deeper network observability is a partner integration.

The seam

Five9 has no parent-company network intelligence platform to lean on. When voice quality degrades, the troubleshooting trail leads to either the carrier (cooperation varies) or a third-party monitoring tool the customer purchased separately.

"Solid product. Same architectural gap."

§ 04 — Four Talking Points You Own

Differentiators that cannot be copied by Microsoft, Zoom, or Genesys.

These four points are structural — not features that a competitor could clone in their next release. They follow from Cisco's ownership of ThousandEyes and the depth of integration into Webex Control Hub and RoomOS.

I.
Network Path data inside the Webex Troubleshooting view.
When a user with the ThousandEyes Endpoint Agent joins a Webex meeting, a Network Path line appears under Audio Quality in their participant details — directly in Control Hub. One click cross-launches into ThousandEyes for hop-by-hop analysis. No tab switching. No CSV export. No third-party tool. Microsoft and Zoom literally cannot replicate this without acquiring an internet intelligence company.
Seller line: "It's not an integration. It's the same screen."
II.
RoomOS firmware ships ThousandEyes as part of the device.
Cisco Board, Desk, and Room series devices contain the ThousandEyes Endpoint Agent in firmware. Activate it from Control Hub with a license. No software install. No agent deployment. No room-by-room rollout. Teams Rooms and Zoom Rooms run on third-party hardware — Logitech, Poly, Neat, Yealink — none of which embed network intelligence at the firmware level. Cisco's vertical hardware/software/observability integration is unmatched in the room.
Seller line: "Every room is already instrumented. Activate and go."
III.
Webex data centers are instrumented from the inside.
ThousandEyes operates Webex-specific Cloud Agents at the front door of Webex Meetings and Webex Calling data centers globally. The traffic destination has measurement points; the source has measurement points; the path between them is therefore observable bidirectionally. No competitor has equivalent presence inside their own delivery infrastructure paired with global ISP-side vantage points. This is uniquely Cisco's network primacy compounding into a Cisco collab advantage.
Seller line: "Both ends of the call are watched. By the same platform."
IV.
Contact Center calls get brand-grade evidence when something goes wrong.
A Webex Contact Center call has stakes: it's the customer talking to the brand. When that call degrades, "we'll look into it" isn't an answer. With ThousandEyes embedded, supervisors can isolate within minutes whether it's the agent's home Wi-Fi, the agent's ISP, the carrier transit, or the Webex Calling DC. Genesys, NICE, and Five9 customers wait for third-party tools to investigate — or for the carrier to admit fault. Cisco closes the loop in real time.
Seller line: "Your brand's voice quality, defended in minutes — not days."
§ 05 — Objection Handling

What they'll push back with. And what you say next.

Customers entrenched with Microsoft, Zoom, or Genesys will raise legitimate counter-arguments — sunk cost, "good enough" monitoring, separate observability tools. Here's how to respond without over-promising and without retreating.

Tap a scenario. The prospect's actual likely objection on the left, and the response you should actually give on the right.
Prospect · IT Director
"We already pay for M365. Teams comes free. Why would I pay extra for Webex when the meeting app does the same thing?"
Your Response
Reframe from "meeting app" to "meeting platform." Teams costs nothing — until your CFO's investor call drops, and you can't prove whether it was your network, your ISP, or Microsoft's cloud. Then you're buying IR or Nectar to find out, on top of M365. Webex with ThousandEyes ships that capability natively. The honest comparison is M365 + Teams + Nectar versus Webex + ThousandEyes — and the math changes fast.
"Free at the app layer means expensive at the observability layer."
Prospect · Network Architect
"We use Microsoft's Call Quality Dashboard. It tells us when calls are bad. What more do we need?"
Your Response
Acknowledge CQD's strengths, then surface the question it can't answer. CQD is genuinely useful — for telling you a call was bad. It cannot tell you why, beyond what the Teams client itself observed. When the next finger-pointing call happens with your ISP or with Microsoft, you have client telemetry; ThousandEyes gives you path-level evidence. One tells you the symptom. The other tells you the cause.
"CQD says the call was bad. ThousandEyes says exactly which hop made it bad."
Prospect · CIO
"Zoom works. Our users like it. Why would we go through a migration to Webex?"
Your Response
Don't argue with what works. Reframe what's at stake. Zoom-the-meeting-app is fine. Zoom-the-platform leaves you blind every time the network underneath misbehaves. Most enterprises don't notice until something visible breaks — a board meeting, an analyst call, a contact center day. By that point, you're chasing root cause with tools you don't own. Webex isn't trying to be a better Zoom; it's trying to be a more accountable platform. If your stakes are growing, that distinction matters.
"You don't migrate for the meeting. You migrate for the day the meeting breaks."
Prospect · Ops Lead
"We already use Nectar (or IR, or VOSS) for unified communications observability. Why is Cisco's better?"
Your Response
Respect the tool — they exist for good reason — and then surface what's missing. Those vendors are excellent. They sell observability into the gap that Microsoft, Zoom, and Genesys leave open. With Webex, that gap doesn't exist — the observability is built in. So you have a choice: keep paying for the workaround your current vendor's gap creates, or buy a platform where the workaround isn't necessary. One of those scales better as your environment grows.
"Those tools are great. They also exist because your current vendor needs them."
Prospect · CX Leader
"Genesys is the contact center leader. Why would I pick Webex CC over them?"
Your Response
Don't fight on the CX feature war — fight on what protects the brand. Genesys has a great platform. Their AI is competitive. Their omnichannel is mature. Where they lose is the moment voice quality degrades on a customer call, because their answer is "buy Operata or Nectar to find out why." Cisco's answer is "Control Hub already shows you why." For a CX leader, the brand-defending capability is in the network, not just the app. Webex CC with ThousandEyes is the only platform where you can prove "it's not us" before the customer hangs up.
"Genesys protects your contact center. Webex protects your brand."
Prospect · Procurement
"Tying Webex to ThousandEyes sounds like Cisco creating lock-in. Can I use ThousandEyes without Webex, or vice versa?"
Your Response
Answer truthfully — this is a strength, not a weakness. Yes, both products run standalone. ThousandEyes monitors Microsoft Teams, Zoom, AWS, Salesforce — anyone's stack. Webex runs on any network. The integration is a deployment advantage, not a coupling mechanism. Compare to Microsoft, where the observability story requires you to commit to M365 entirely; or Genesys, where you must adopt their partner ecosystem. Cisco's stack is composable — but if you choose to compose Cisco-on-Cisco, the integration depth is the reward.
"You can buy Webex alone. You can buy ThousandEyes alone. Bought together, they multiply."

§ 07 — Rapid-Fire FAQ

The quick ones. Click to reveal.

Answers to questions that come up in the last five minutes of a call, when the customer is deciding whether to schedule a second meeting.

"Which Cisco devices support the embedded ThousandEyes agent?"
+
The answer

Cisco Board, Desk, and Room Series collaboration devices — running RoomOS — have the ThousandEyes Agent built into the firmware. Activation is via Control Hub, requires an Endpoint Agent license per device, and does not work in IPv6-only environments or with FIPS Mode enabled.

Sourcehelp.webex.com — ThousandEyes Agent integration for Board, Desk, and Room Series devices
"What's required to see Network Path data inside Webex Control Hub?"
+
The answer

Three things: (1) a ThousandEyes Endpoint Agent installed on the user's machine (version 1.120.0 or higher), (2) Webex Desktop App locally signed in, and (3) the Control Hub integration activated. Once configured, a Network Path line appears under Audio Quality in the participant details page for any meeting that user joins.

Sourcehelp.webex.com — Integrate ThousandEyes with Troubleshooting in Control Hub
"Can ThousandEyes monitor Webex Contact Center agent calls specifically?"
+
The answer

Yes. ThousandEyes Cloud Agents are deployed at Webex Calling data centers and capture RTP traffic bidirectionally, so the path between agent and Webex Calling DC is observable. For WFH agents, the Endpoint Agent on the agent's laptop closes the loop on home Wi-Fi, ISP, and last-mile conditions.

Sourcethousandeyes.com — Webex Monitoring solution page
"Does Endpoint Agent monitor non-Cisco meetings (Teams, Zoom)?"
+
The answer

Yes — the agent itself is platform-agnostic and can monitor Teams and Zoom traffic. The key differentiator is integration: for Webex, the data appears natively inside Webex Control Hub. For Teams and Zoom, the data appears only in the ThousandEyes app — there's no native surfacing inside the Microsoft Teams Admin Center or Zoom Dashboard, because those vendors don't support it.

SourceWWT analysis — ThousandEyes integration with Webex Suite and Contact Centers
"What licensing is required for the Webex side?"
+
The answer

The ThousandEyes integration is available across Webex licensing tiers but the Endpoint Agent licenses (Essential or Advantage, one per device) are purchased separately. For Contact Center customers, Cisco AI Assistant for Webex Contact Center is a paid add-on that pairs naturally with the ThousandEyes story for full agent + supervisor + network visibility.

"How is this different from buying Webex and ThousandEyes separately?"
+
The answer

The products are licensed separately, but the integration is unique to Cisco-owned products. You cannot replicate this experience by buying a competitor's UCaaS plus ThousandEyes. ThousandEyes data won't surface inside Microsoft Teams Admin Center, Zoom Dashboard, or Genesys Cloud. The native cross-launch, the embedded RoomOS agent, and the Webex DC Cloud Agents are exclusively Cisco-on-Cisco capabilities.

"Can MSPs and partners deploy this for customers?"

The answer

Yes. Partners managing customer Control Hub organizations can mass-activate Endpoint Agents on RoomOS devices and link account groups across customer tenants. This is a strong MSP enablement story — particularly for partners selling managed Webex services who want differentiated value beyond just provisioning.

"Is there a way to demo this in a customer call?"
+
The answer

Yes — and you should. A live demo of Webex Control Hub Troubleshooting with the Network Path data visible, then a one-click cross-launch into ThousandEyes path visualization, is the most persuasive moment in this entire sales motion. § 08 — How To Run The Play

Four moves. In this order.

A concrete seller motion. This flow has been observed to convert competitive Webex deals — particularly in accounts where Microsoft or Zoom have strong incumbency — by shifting the comparison axis to network observability.

Move 01 · Discover
Find the last bad meeting that mattered.
Don't pitch ThousandEyes. Ask about their last brand-impacting communication failure. A board meeting that glitched. A contact center afternoon when calls dropped. How did they figure out what went wrong?
"Tell me about the last time a Webex / Teams / Zoom meeting failed at a moment that really mattered — and how you figured out why."
Move 02 · Diagnose
Surface the observability gap.
Without naming products, show the customer that their current toolchain answers "was it bad?" but not "why was it bad and is it our fault?" Establish that this gap is a structural feature of their current vendor's architecture.
"Your current platform tells you the call was bad. What tells you whether the cause was your network, your ISP, or the vendor's cloud?"
Move 03 · Demonstrate
Show the cross-launch in Control Hub.
Open Webex Control Hub Troubleshooting. Show a meeting with Network Path data. Click into ThousandEyes for the hop-by-hop view. Then ask the customer where, in their current platform, they'd see this. They can't.
"Watch this. From the meeting, into the network, in two clicks. Let me know when you've seen the equivalent elsewhere."
Move 04 · Deploy POV
Activate on a pilot.
Offer a 30-day Webex pilot with ThousandEyes Endpoint Agents on a subset of users (10–50). Run a board call or town hall during the pilot. The data from that real moment closes the deal — every time.
"Give me 30 days and 25 users. Run your next exec event on the pilot. We'll review the network data together."
