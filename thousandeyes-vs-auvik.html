§ 01 — The Positioning Divide

Different categories, mistaken for competitors.

Gartner classifies these tools in separate markets for a reason. Auvik lives in the Network Monitoring / MSP tooling space. ThousandEyes leads the Digital Experience Monitoring category. Understanding that distinction is the entire argument.

Auvik
Network Mgmt · MSP
"Real-time mapping, device monitoring, and config backup for networks you administer."
  • AudienceMSPs and internal IT managing owned LAN / branch infrastructure.
  • ScopeSNMP / syslog / config. Stops at the WAN demarc.
  • PricingPer billable device (routers, switches, firewalls, WLCs).
  • Sweet SpotSmall-to-mid networks with predictable device counts.
  • Blind SpotThe internet itself — ISPs, SaaS, CDNs, BGP, cloud regions.
ThousandEyes
DEM · Internet Intelligence
"End-to-end visibility across every network your business depends on — owned or not."
  • AudienceEnterprises, carriers, SaaS providers, financial institutions, government.
  • ScopeUser → LAN → WAN → ISP → Internet core → Cloud → App.
  • PricingUnit-based; value scales with enterprise footprint.
  • Sweet SpotHybrid, SaaS-heavy, globally distributed environments.
  • Blind SpotIntentionally thin on SNMP device administration (not its purpose).
§ 02 — Architectural DNA

Polling boxes versus measuring the internet.

Auvik's architecture is a hub that SNMP-polls the devices inside your perimeter. ThousandEyes' architecture is a distributed measurement mesh — thousands of agents across hundreds of cities continuously probing paths, routes, and applications from the outside in.

Approach · Perimeter-Bound

Auvik — Inside Your Walls

A single collector appliance polls SNMP inside the LAN. Excellent for device inventory, config drift, and switchport diagnostics. Visibility ends at the edge router — everything past it is a black box.

Approach · Globally Distributed

ThousandEyes — The Internet Is the Network

Four agent types measure continuously from everywhere at once. Cloud Agents in 271 cities. Enterprise Agents inside your network. Endpoint Agents on user laptops. Device Agents on CPE. Every hop, every path, every ISP — recorded and correlated.

§ 05 — The Same Incident, Two Tools

Four real-world scenarios. Watch what each tool can say.

Theory is cheap. Here are four incidents that occur routinely in enterprise IT. Each tab shows what the tool can actually prove about the incident — and, just as importantly, what it can't.

Scenario · 08:42 EST

"Teams calls keep dropping across the Atlanta office."

IT receives a wave of tickets. Calls are choppy, video freezes, screen shares stall. The on-prem switches are green. The WAN router reports no errors. So is it Microsoft? Is it the ISP? Is it Wi-Fi? Is it the user's laptop?

The pressure: a VP is about to join a board presentation. Someone needs an answer in minutes, not hours.
Auvik says
  • Switch and router health look normal.
  • Bandwidth utilization is within baseline.
  • No device is alerting.
  • That's… all.
Verdict: Confirmed the LAN is fine. Did not reveal where the problem actually is.
ThousandEyes says
  • Endpoint Agent shows 6% packet loss on the ISP's second hop.
  • Path visualization isolates loss to a single upstream peer.
  • Teams service itself is healthy globally.
  • Evidence is exportable to the ISP for escalation.
Verdict: Root cause in minutes, with evidence your carrier cannot argue with.
Scenario · 10:15 EST

"Remote workers can't reach internal apps through the VPN."

Help desk tickets are piling up from three different regions. The VPN concentrator shows active tunnels. Some users reconnect and it works; others stay broken. Internal apps respond fine when tested from HQ.

Is this an ISP issue for some users? A routing asymmetry? A problem at the concentrator's upstream? A DNS quirk?
Auvik says
  • VPN concentrator device is up.
  • Session counts look nominal.
  • No visibility into remote users' networks.
  • No visibility into the path between user and concentrator.
Verdict: Blind to everything outside your building.
ThousandEyes says
  • Endpoint Agents from affected users show MTU fragmentation on one ISP.
  • Cloud Agents confirm the concentrator is globally reachable.
  • Path viz exposes an asymmetric route via a congested transit provider.
  • Correlates VPN issues to a single ISP — not to your infrastructure.
Verdict: You know exactly who to call — and it isn't you.
Scenario · 02:07 EST

"A critical SaaS vendor has a regional outage — but won't admit it."

Your order-management SaaS is sluggish. The vendor's status page is green. Half your regions report failures; half don't. The CIO wants to know whether to fail over to a secondary system — a decision that costs millions either way.

Without independent evidence, you're taking the vendor's word for it at the worst possible moment.
Auvik says
  • Your on-prem network is healthy.
  • No SaaS-side visibility.
  • No BGP or DNS insight.
  • You're dependent on the vendor's self-reporting.
Verdict: Cannot participate in the decision at all.
ThousandEyes says
  • Cloud Agents in 12 regions confirm degradation in 4 specific ones.
  • BGP data shows a stuck route affecting the vendor's AS.
  • Internet Outages map correlates the pattern to a known transit failure.
  • Failover decision is now evidence-based, not a gamble.
Verdict: You saw it before the vendor's status page did — and you can prove it.
Scenario · 14:30 EST

"A small branch office just lost connectivity."

A 15-person regional sales office drops off the network. The branch router has gone dark. Someone needs to know: is the router dead? Is the circuit down? Is it the ISP? Is there a physical issue in the closet?

This is the bread-and-butter scenario Auvik was designed for — and it shows.
Auvik says
  • Router and switch at the site are unreachable.
  • Last-seen config snapshot is available for restore.
  • Historical device health points to likely hardware cause.
  • You have documentation to hand to the tech on-site.
Verdict: A legitimately strong scenario for Auvik — remote device diagnostics are its core competence.
ThousandEyes says
  • Cloud Agents confirm the branch ISP circuit is down externally.
  • BGP shows the branch's prefix is no longer being announced.
  • You know the problem is upstream, not your equipment.
  • Evidence is ready to hand to the ISP on the first call.
Verdict: Still wins on root-cause confirmation, but this is Auvik's strongest showing.

Strategic Fit

Auvik was built for MSPs billing per device. ThousandEyes was built for organizations whose revenue, customer trust, and employee productivity depend on digital experiences they don't directly control. Match the tool to the stakes.

When Auvik is the right answer

An MSP managing dozens of small clients. An internal team with predictable, on-prem infrastructure and a device-centric operations model. A shop that needs config backup, automated discovery, and a dashboard for the NOC more than it needs end-to-end experience visibility. Auvik is good at this. Genuinely.

When ThousandEyes is the right answer

Any organization where a slow SaaS app, a misrouted BGP prefix, a noisy ISP, or a degraded CDN can cost real money or real customers. Hybrid workforces. Cloud-native architectures. Multi-region footprints. Anyone whose users log in over the public internet to do their jobs — which, in 2026, is nearly everyone.
