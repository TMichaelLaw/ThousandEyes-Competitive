§ 02 — Know The Field

Four competitors, four DEM stories. Each has a real seam.

Walk in respecting what each vendor has built. Then know the seam. Each competitor's DEM is technically credible — and each has a specific blind spot or trade-off you can pressure-test without being dismissive.

Fortinet
FortiGate + FortiMonitor
SaaS-based DEM · Synthetic + endpoint
What it is

FortiMonitor is a legitimate SaaS DEM acquired (via Panopta) and integrated into Fortinet's Security Fabric. Does synthetic transaction monitoring, DNS/TLS/RTT metrics, SD-WAN underlay monitoring.

The seam

FortiMonitor is an acquisition still being knitted into the broader fabric. Synthetic tests run from Fortinet's probes, not a global, independent vantage network. No BGP route intelligence. Less trusted as a third-party escalation source with ISPs.

"Good telemetry inside Fortinet's world — thin outside it."
HPE Aruba
EdgeConnect SD-WAN
DEM inside Unified SASE
What it is

HPE Aruba bundles DEM into its EdgeConnect SASE platform alongside ZTNA, SWG, and CASB. Offers real-time path monitoring, per-underlay loss/latency/jitter, and application identification.

The seam

DEM is tied to the Unified SASE subscription — most compelling when customer buys the full HPE Aruba stack. Stronger on appliance and uplink telemetry than on global internet intelligence. No native BGP monitoring, no independent global vantage mesh.

"Credible SASE-native DEM. Not an internet intelligence platform."
Palo Alto
Prisma SD-WAN + ADEM
Autonomous DEM · SASE-native
What it is

ADEM is the strongest DEM story among rivals — built into Prisma SASE, GlobalProtect, and Prisma SD-WAN (ION) devices. Endpoint agents, synthetic tests, hop-by-hop path analysis across overlay and underlay. Recently extended via Universal Agents to non-Palo branches.

The seam

ADEM is excellent inside the Prisma Access / SASE funnel. Its external internet visibility is indirect — no BGP route intelligence, no independent global collector network, no equivalent of ThousandEyes' Internet Outages Map. DEM is a licensed add-on, not a natively-running agent.

"The closest rival. Still vendor-internal where it matters most."
Versa
Versa Secure SD-WAN
Versa DEM · Unified SASE
What it is

Versa DEM runs on the Versa Client and VCG/CPE, exports metrics to Versa Analytics, produces a 1-100 DEM rank per user/device. Covers Wi-Fi, local, SD-WAN, internet, app segments.

The seam

Entirely inside Versa's Analytics engine — strong on user experience scoring, weaker on independent external validation. No BGP monitoring. No global cloud agent mesh. Smaller installed base of measurement points than ThousandEyes or even ADEM.

"Strong analytics. Small measurement footprint outside its own gateways."

§ 04 — Four Talking Points You Own

The differentiators that don't have an equivalent on the other side.

These four points are what the competitor simply cannot match at feature parity. When you're in a deal and need to create genuine separation, these are the weapons. They also happen to be verifiable, not marketing.

I.
The measurement mesh your customer's network is already part of.
ThousandEyes' 271-city Cloud Agent network has been measuring the public internet since 2010. Every new Meraki MX site plugs into that existing mesh. Your customer instantly benefits from the same vantage points that Netflix, Microsoft, and the Fortune 100 use to validate their internet experience. No competitor has a network of this scale or age.
Seller line: "The internet's memory is already on our side."
II.
BGP and DNS are monitored natively — not as an afterthought.
When the problem is a stuck BGP route affecting your customer's M365 tenant, or a DNS hijack impacting their payment processor, ThousandEyes sees it. Competitor DEMs are blind to these categories entirely. This matters because 40–60% of impactful "is it us?" outages in 2026 trace back to BGP, DNS, or carrier-layer events, not to device failures.
Seller line: "When the internet breaks, we saw it first."
III.
Independence from the SD-WAN vendor is the feature.
When a customer's board asks "are you sure it's not our SD-WAN vendor's problem?", ThousandEyes' data carries weight precisely because its measurement methodology predates the Cisco acquisition and remains the third-party industry standard. Competitor DEMs are, by definition, the vendor grading its own homework.
Seller line: "We're the only SD-WAN that ships its own auditor."
IV.
The deployment experience is genuinely trivial.
On MX67 or higher, running firmware 18.104+, activation is a checkbox in the Meraki dashboard. The Enterprise Agent runs in a container inside MX firmware, uses its own internal stack, and doesn't touch data-plane traffic. Compare to standing up FortiMonitor collectors, licensing ADEM add-ons, or deploying Versa Clients — this is a morning, not a project plan.
Seller line: "Checkbox. Done. Monitoring live."
§ 05 — Objection Handling

What they'll push back with. And what you say next.

The customer has done their homework. They will raise legitimate counter-arguments — pricing, vendor lock-in, overlap with tools they already own. Here's how to respond without over-promising and without retreating.

Tap a scenario. Each one has the customer's actual likely objection on the left, and the response you should actually give on the right.
Prospect · CIO
"Our Fortinet quote is 25% cheaper. Meraki's already a premium — why should I pay more for another layer?"
Your Response
Reframe from hardware cost to incident cost. One severe SaaS degradation — a sluggish M365 quarter-close, a Salesforce failure during peak — runs six figures in lost productivity and IT time. ThousandEyes isn't "another layer" — it's the one capability that turns a 4-hour war room into a 20-minute root-cause confirmation with evidence you can hand to the ISP.
"Your Fortinet savings disappear on the first major outage where you can't prove it wasn't you."
Prospect · Network Director
"We already use FortiMonitor. It does synthetic tests, it does SD-WAN monitoring. What's different?"
Your Response
Agree, then draw the line. FortiMonitor is a capable tool inside Fortinet's fabric. But ask two questions: "When the ISP blames you, whose data do you show them — Fortinet's, or the internet's independent standard?" and "Does FortiMonitor tell you when BGP announces a stuck route affecting your tenant, or when a CDN degrades for a region you care about?"
"FortiMonitor watches your network. ThousandEyes watches the internet. You need both — and we ship one of them in the box."
Prospect · CTO
"Palo Alto tells me ADEM does hop-by-hop path analysis, endpoint agents, synthetic tests. Isn't that the same thing?"
Your Response
Acknowledge ADEM's real strength — then expose the scope. ADEM is the closest rival — don't dismiss it. But ADEM's visibility is optimized for traffic that traverses Prisma Access. It's Palo Alto watching Palo Alto's SASE delivery path. ThousandEyes runs independent measurement across the entire public internet — regardless of whether traffic touches Cisco infrastructure. And critically, ADEM has no BGP monitoring and no global outage map.
"ADEM tells you about Prisma's delivery. ThousandEyes tells you about the whole internet — including Prisma."
Prospect · Platform Lead
"We already use Datadog / New Relic for observability. Isn't ThousandEyes redundant?"
Your Response
Draw the distinction cleanly. Datadog and New Relic excel at APM — what's happening inside your applications and code. ThousandEyes tells you what's happening between the user and the app — the network, ISP, BGP, DNS layer. They're complementary, not overlapping. Datadog tells you a request took 800ms; ThousandEyes tells you 600ms of that was a congested transit provider in São Paulo.
"APM answers 'why is my app slow.' ThousandEyes answers 'is it even my app's fault.'"
Prospect · Procurement
"Cisco buying ThousandEyes makes this sound like lock-in. Can we use it without Meraki MX?"
Your Response
Answer truthfully — this is a strength. Yes, absolutely. ThousandEyes runs standalone on Cisco ISR/ASR, Catalyst 8000, as a standalone Enterprise Agent on Linux, in VMs, and has non-Cisco customers across every industry. The Meraki MX integration is a zero-friction deployment advantage — not a lock-in mechanism. It's exactly the opposite of the Palo Alto model, where ADEM is tightly coupled to Prisma.
"The integration makes adoption easy; the product itself is completely portable."
Prospect · IT Director
"What license do we actually need? I've heard ThousandEyes is gated behind premium SD-WAN tiers."
Your Response
Be precise — customers hate vague license answers. Native ThousandEyes on Meraki MX requires either Secure SD-WAN Plus (which includes ThousandEyes test units as part of the subscription) or Advanced Security (which enables the integration; test units can be added as needed). Supported on MX67 and above, firmware 18.104+. Activation is in the Meraki dashboard under the Insight tab.
"Plus tier — it's built in. Advanced Security — it's one add-on away. Either way, no separate procurement cycle."

§ 07 — Rapid-Fire FAQ

The quick ones. Click to reveal.

Answers to the questions that tend to come up in the last five minutes of a call, when the customer is deciding whether to schedule a second meeting.

"Which Meraki MX models support this?"
+
The answer

MX67 and above, running firmware 18.104 or later. Older MX models don't have container resources for the Enterprise Agent. If the customer is on an older MX, position this as part of the refresh conversation — the integration is genuinely a refresh driver.

SourceMeraki MX ThousandEyes Configuration Guide · documentation.meraki.com
"Does the agent interfere with data-plane traffic?"
+
The answer

No. The Enterprise Agent uses the MX's internal networking stack (198.51.x.x reserved space) and doesn't touch customer traffic unless it's specifically testing a LAN-side server or calling back to the ThousandEyes dashboard. This is a deliberate architectural choice documented by the product team.

SourceMeraki + ThousandEyes Integration Troubleshooting Guide · documentation.meraki.com
"Do customers have to set up a new ThousandEyes account?"
+
The answer

If they don't have one, the onboarding flow creates and links the account group as part of first activation. If they already use ThousandEyes, the integration links the existing account group. It's one guided workflow either way, inside the Meraki dashboard.

"Can MSPs deploy this on behalf of customers?"
+
The answer

Yes — this is a key MSP story. Partners can mass-activate agents across all customer organizations they manage in the Meraki dashboard, with per-customer ThousandEyes account linkage. For MSPs, this eliminates the previous burden of standing up dedicated VM-based agents per customer network.

"What's the pre-built test template library cover?"
+
The answer

Out of the box: Microsoft 365, Salesforce, Webex by Cisco, Zoom, Google Workspace, and common web properties. Custom tests for internal apps are configurable via the ThousandEyes dashboard after activation. For sales demos, the M365 template is the most universally resonant.

"How does this compare to standalone ThousandEyes?"
+
The answer

Same product, same dashboard, same data pipeline. The MX-native deployment uses a purpose-built version of the Enterprise Agent engineered to run in the MX's container environment. Feature parity for active monitoring tests is effectively complete; customers who need BGP test types specifically still need a traditional BGP monitor, but that's rare for branch-site use cases.

"Can we do a proof of value before a commercial decision?"
+
The answer

Yes. Trial accounts for Advanced Security and SD-WAN Plus include the ThousandEyes integration — customers can activate on trial MX devices and generate real data inside their own environment before committing. This is a powerful POV motion: activate, run for two weeks, and the data will frequently itself produce the buying case.

§ 08 — How To Run The Play

Four moves. In this order.

A concrete seller motion. This flow has been observed to convert discovery into committed POV inside two meetings for mid-market and enterprise accounts alike.

Move 01 · Discover
Find the last painful outage.
Don't pitch DEM. Ask about their last major "is it us or them?" incident. How long did it take to isolate? Who was in the war room? Was the ISP helpful?
"Tell me about the last time your team spent more than an hour on a finger-pointing call with a carrier or SaaS vendor."
Move 02 · Diagnose
Surface the scope gap.
Without naming products, surface the capability they're missing: independent internet intelligence. Use the landscape framework above — validate their current tooling, then show where it ends.
"Your current stack tells you your equipment is healthy. What tells you the internet itself isn't the problem?"
Move 03 · Demonstrate
Show the dashboard, not the slideware.
A live Meraki dashboard demo with ThousandEyes Insight tab activated is worth twenty minutes of slides. Show one-click activation, the test template library, and a path-viz of an M365 test.
"Let me just toggle this on in a lab account and show you — 90 seconds."
Move 04 · Deploy POV
Activate on their trial.
Don't wait for a formal procurement. Offer a zero-cost activation on trial licenses for 2-4 weeks. Come back with real data from their environment. The data closes the deal.
"Give me two weeks on a trial license. I'll come back with a report on what's actually happening to your M365 traffic right now."
